01. The Philosophy

Everything in this playbook comes from one place: we succeed when we understand the problem better than the client does.

We are not here to convince. We are here to diagnose. The ones who move are the ones who already know, in their gut, that they need Korea. Our job is to crystallize that gut feeling into a clear path forward.

Seven principles guide this entire approach.

Principle 1: Hormozi's Value Equation

Value = (Quality of Offer + Clarity + Scarcity of Opportunity) / (Time to Implementation + Effort Required + Skepticism Friction)

Everything we do increases the numerator and decreases the denominator. Better insight drops increase perceived value. Clear outcomes and timelines reduce friction. Scarcity of the Korea window decreases perceived friction.

Principle 2: Give Away Secrets

Every discovery call includes 2-3 insight drops. These insights are diagnostic. They show we understand the market, the category, the competitive landscape, the cultural nuances. The ones who get it will feel like we are the only ones who see clearly. That feeling earns the right to propose.

Principle 3: Chet Holmes' Pyramid

Most of your market is in education mode. Maybe 3% are ready to buy right now. Your job is not to convert that 3%. It is to move people up the pyramid. From 'I don't know this exists' to 'I know this exists' to 'I'm considering it' to 'I'm ready.' This playbook is structured around that movement.

Principle 4: Priestley's Oversubscribed

Jdot operates as if HoloHive is booked up. We don't chase. We qualify. We have done this before. We know what works. Teams that are ready move fast. Teams that aren't don't. We respect both.

Principle 5: Blair Enns' Win Without Pitching

A pitch is a presentation. A pitch assumes they need convincing. We do diagnosis instead. We ask the questions they should have already asked themselves. When they sit with those questions for a moment, they realize we are the only ones asking them. That realization IS the sale.

Principle 6: Hormozi's CLOSER Framework

Every call follows this structure:

Clarify. Ask the right diagnostic questions. Understand where they are and where they want to be.

Label. Reflect back what you heard. Show them you understand.

Overview. Share the market reality. Plant insight drops. Show them the opportunity.

Sell the Vacation. Paint the picture of what success looks like. Not the tasks. The destination.

Explain Away Concerns. Preempt objections. Address the bear market. Acknowledge the risk. Show why now still makes sense.

Reinforce the Decision. Book the next meeting. Momentum only lives in the booked meeting.

Principle 7: Expert Diagnosis, Not Free Consulting

The discovery call is diagnostic. Share WHAT is happening. Why the Korea window is closing. Why this category is moving. Why their competitors are positioned differently. That is diagnosis.

Keep the HOW for the engagement. The strategy. The specific campaigns. The KOL strategy for their token. The community playbook. That is what they pay for.

The moment you start explaining tactics, you become a consultant giving away the value. You become one of the agencies they're comparing. Stay in diagnosis mode.

What This Means Practically

  • Before every call, Jdot knows what insights to drop. Not generic. Specific to the project.
  • Every message sounds human. Casual but knowledgeable. Not corporate. Not salesy.
  • Every call ends with a booked next meeting. BAMFAM. Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Momentum dies the moment a call ends without a follow-up booked.
  • Objections are prevented, not fought. If Jdot runs the CLOSER framework correctly, 70% of objections never come up.
  • Scarcity is real. The Korea window is actually closing. We are not making this up. This is the only sales tactic that works in this playbook because it is true.

02. The Three Lead Buckets

Not all qualified leads are equally ready. The difference between a Bucket A and a Bucket B is weeks. Maybe a month. So we treat them differently.

The question that sorts them: If I offered you to start next week, would you say yes?

Bucket A: Ready Now

These leads are warm. They know Korea matters. They have allocated budget. They want to move but they don't know who to trust or how to start.

Characteristics:

  • Mentioned Korea unprompted
  • Asked about timeline and pricing on the first call
  • Budget already approved or available
  • Decision-maker is on the call
  • No significant blockers (no 'need to talk to my team')

Jdot's move:

  • Discovery call happens Tuesday or Wednesday
  • Proposal call happens that Friday or the following Monday
  • Contract signed by mid-week or the following week
  • Work starts within 1-2 weeks

Bucket B: Interested, Not Ready

These leads are warm but not hot. They see Korea as strategic but there is friction. Budget is there but needs approval. Team is skeptical. Timing might be off.

Characteristics:

  • Asked good questions on discovery call
  • Saw the value in the insight drops
  • Need to talk to their team or partner
  • Budget exists but might be allocated elsewhere
  • Market conditions make them hesitant

Jdot's move:

  • Discovery call to map the real blocker
  • A 2-3 week nurture sequence with market data and social proof
  • Soft re-engagement when the blocker changes (market shifts, competitor moves, etc.)
  • Proposal call when they're warm

Bucket C: Early Stage / Exploratory

These leads are cold. Korea is not yet on the priority list. They are checking. They are curious. But they are not ready. No timeline. No budget allocated. No real decision-making pressure.

Characteristics:

  • Asked about Korea out of curiosity, not conviction
  • No budget allocated
  • Team hasn't discussed Korea at all
  • Timing unclear
  • Someone said 'maybe later'

Jdot's move:

  • No proposal call
  • Monthly value touches
  • Watch for trigger events: competitor moves, market shift, new funding, new team hire
  • When a trigger fires, Jdot re-engages and moves them up to Bucket B

The Decision Framework

At the end of every discovery call, Jdot knows which bucket they are in. This determines what happens next.

  • Bucket A. Book proposal call before hanging up.
  • Bucket B. Book a data walkthrough before hanging up. This is different. Not a proposal. Just value sharing.
  • Bucket C. No next meeting booked. Monthly nurture only.

03. Pre-Call Preparation

The 10 minutes before a discovery call matter more than most people think. Jdot's natural expertise is the weapon. Preparation loads that weapon.

Research Checklist (10 min before every call)

Before Jdot gets on a discovery call, Phil sends a quick research document covering:

  • Project basics. What do they do? What stage? Pre-TGE, post-TGE, or established?
  • Token status. Launched? Price trajectory? Volume distribution by region?
  • Recent news. Last 2 weeks of announcements, tweets, partnerships.
  • Funding. How much raised? From who? When?
  • Team. Who are the decision-makers? Is the person on the call the one who signs?
  • Competitors. Are any of their competitors already in Korea?
  • Korea presence. Do they already have any Korean community, KOLs, or exchange listings?
  • Prepared insight drops. 2-3 tailored insights based on the above.
  • Case study ready. Have a relevant example loaded and ready to reference.
  • Call objective clear. Bucket A close? Bucket B nurture setup? Or exploratory?

Decision-Maker Mapping

Before the call, try to figure out who makes the decision on a $30-100K spend. If the person on the call isn't the decision-maker, your discovery call strategy shifts. Instead of closing, you are building an internal champion.

Early in the call, ask: 'Walk me through how your team makes decisions on new partners or vendors. Is this something you'd decide, or does it go through a team review?'

This one question saves weeks of back-and-forth later.

Agenda Setting (Send 24-48 Hours Before)

Phil sends this message via TG:

Phil
Message:
hey [name]. looking forward to our call [day]. here's what i'm thinking for our time together: i want to understand where you're at with Korea, share a few things i'm seeing in the market that might be relevant, and if it makes sense we can talk about what working together could look like. if there's anything specific you want to cover, let me know.

Why this works. Sets expectations. Positions Jdot as the one driving the agenda (frame control). Gives them permission to add their own topics. Makes the call feel structured, not salesy.

04. Discovery Call Script and Framework

The discovery call is the most important touchpoint in the entire process. It is where trust is built or broken. This framework is built on Hormozi's CLOSER structure adapted for HoloHive.

The Discovery Call Has 5 Jobs

  1. Diagnose their Korea situation. Where they are, where they want to be, what is blocking them.
  2. Demonstrate expertise through diagnosis, not free consulting. Show you understand their problem at a level they did not expect.
  3. Plant repeatable soundbites they will share with their team after the call.
  4. Map the decision-making process. Who decides? What is the timeline?
  5. Book the next meeting before hanging up. BAMFAM.

Phase 1: Open and Agenda (2 min)

What Jdot says:

Jdot
Opening:
Appreciate you jumping on. Before we dive in, here is what I am thinking. I want to understand where you are at with Korea, share a few things I am seeing in the market for [their category], and if it makes sense we can talk about next steps. Sound good? Anything you want to make sure we cover?

Why. Sets the frame. Jdot owns the agenda. Gives them space to add topics.

Phase 2: Clarify and Diagnose (8-12 min)

Run the SPIN questions adapted for Korea context.

Situation Questions

  • Tell me about your current community strategy.
  • How are you currently building presence outside the US?
  • What regions or markets are on your radar right now?

Problem Questions

  • What is the biggest challenge you are facing in non-US markets?
  • How has the bear market affected your community building plans?
  • What would change for you if one of your competitors had significant Korea presence and you did not?

Implication Questions

  • So if you had strong Korea presence, what would that look like for your token metrics?
  • If you did not build this in the next 6 months, what happens when competition increases?

Need-Payoff Questions

  • What would be valuable about having a Korea strategy that actually moves the needle?
  • What would success look like in Korea for your project?

Decision-Maker Question

  • Quick question on logistics. If Korea made sense for you, walk me through how that decision would get made on your end. Is this your call, or does it go through a team review?

Phase 3: The Insight Drop (5-8 min)

Critical Rule: Share 2-3 diagnostic insights, not free strategy. Show them WHAT is happening (diagnosis). Keep the HOW (implementation strategy) for the paid engagement. You are proving you understand their world better than anyone. That is what earns the right to propose.

Five insight drop types:

Competitor Intel

Example:
Jdot:
I actually know [competitor]. They signed with a Korean agency last month. Already running KOL campaigns. About 60 days ahead of where you would be starting.

Market Data

Example:
Jdot:
Korean exchanges process more spot volume for [their category] tokens than any other region. The volume opportunity alone is significant for a token like yours.

Exchange Intel

Example:
Jdot:
Upbit and Bithumb are actively looking for [their category] projects right now. The listing window is favorable, which isn't always the case.

Cultural Insight

Example:
Jdot:
Korean crypto community moves differently than Western markets. When a KOL picks up a project, the community follows fast. But if you try to enter without a Korean-speaking team, it backfires.

Client Result

Example:
Jdot:
We ran a campaign for a project similar to yours. Same category, similar stage. Got them [specific metric] in about [timeline].

Soundbite Rule. After each insight, pause. Let them react. Then ask: 'Does that line up with what you are seeing?' or 'How does that land for you?' This forces them to talk themselves through the implication.

Phase 4: The Bridge (2-3 min)

This is between insight drops and close:

Jdot
Bridge:
Based on everything we talked about, here is what I think the path looks like for [their project]. The opportunity in Korea is [specific to their situation]. The timing window is [specific]. And the risk of waiting is [specific competitor or market dynamic].

Why. You are selling the destination (Korea market success), not the plane ticket (HoloHive services). Hormozi's principle: sell the vacation, not the flight.

Phase 5: The BAMFAM Close (2-3 min)

BAMFAM: Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Never end a call without the next meeting on the calendar. This is non-negotiable. The moment a call ends without a next step scheduled, momentum dies.

Bucket A Close

Jdot
Close:
I want to put together a scope and breakdown specific to what we discussed. Let me pull up my calendar. Can you do Thursday afternoon or Friday morning for a 30-min walkthrough?

Note. Don't ask 'would you like to schedule a follow-up?' That is asking for permission to continue selling. Instead, assume the next step and offer two times. Either.or, not yes.no.

Bucket B Close

Jdot
Close:
I am going to send you some case studies and our offer breakdown. I also want to share some data specific to [their category] in Korea. Let us grab 15 min next week so I can walk you through it. Tuesday or Wednesday work?

Note. Even Bucket B gets a booked follow-up. The difference is framing: you are not pitching, you are sharing data. The follow-up is a value delivery, not a sales call. This is BAMFAM in action.

Bucket C Close

Jdot
Close:
Sounds like Korea is on the radar but timing might be early. Totally get it. I will keep you posted on anything relevant to [their space]. When things shift, let us reconnect.

Note. Bucket C is the ONLY bucket where you do not book a next meeting. If they are not ready, pushing a calendar invite feels desperate.

08. Proposal Framework and Pricing

Step 1: Situation Recap

Start the proposal with a summary of what you learned on the discovery call. Show them you listened. Show them you understand.

  • Their current state. Where they are with Korea.
  • Their goal. Where they want to be.
  • The gap. What is blocking them.
  • Why now. The market window and competitive context.

Step 2: The Opportunity

Quantify Korea for them. Not generic. Specific to their token and category.

  • Volume opportunity. Korean exchange volume for their category.
  • Community opportunity. Addressable Korean crypto users in their category.
  • Competitive position. Who is already in Korea. Who is not.
  • Timeline pressure. When the window closes. Why now is better than later.

Step 3: The Scope

Here is what we will do.

  • Community building. Day 1-30.
  • KOL partnerships. Day 15-60.
  • Exchange strategy. Day 30-90.
  • Ongoing content. Day 1-90 and beyond.

Be specific. Not 'we will build community.' Specific actions, specific timeline.

Step 4: Results Roadmap

This is what success looks like.

  • Month 1: [specific metric]. Community size, first KOL partnership, exchange listing.
  • Month 2: [specific metric]. Community growth rate, trading volume lift, sentiment shift.
  • Month 3: [specific metric]. Sustained metrics, foundations for Phase 2.

Step 5: Investment and Terms

State the price. Be clear. No ambiguity.

  • Engagement fee: $[amount].
  • Timeline: 90 days (with 30-day extension available).
  • Payment terms: 50% upfront, 50% at Day 45.
  • What happens after 90 days: Transition plan. Can move to retainer or project-based work.

BAMFAM on the Proposal Call

Every proposal call ends with a booked next step. Period.

If buying signals strong. 'I can send the contract today. Let us grab 15 min on [day] to go through any final questions. [time] work?'

If they need to discuss. 'Who else needs to weigh in? Let me set up a call with them. How is [day]?'

If they need time. 'Take the time you need. Let us touch base [specific day] so I can answer anything that comes up. Does [time] work?'

Notice. every single path ends with a specific day and time. Not 'let me know.' Not 'whenever you are ready.' A specific booked time.

Mutual Action Plan (For $50K+ Deals)

For larger deals or deals with multiple stakeholders, create a simple shared doc that maps the path from 'interested' to 'signed.' Include:

  • Key stakeholders and their roles.
  • Timeline with milestones.
  • What HoloHive provides at each step.
  • What their team provides.
  • Decision date.

This does two things. (1) It makes the deal feel real and organized. (2) It surfaces hidden blockers early. If they cannot fill in the stakeholder section, you know the decision-maker mapping is not done yet.

Scarcity and the Korea Window

Mention it. Do not oversell it. The Korea window is real.

  • KOL prices are lower in a bear market. They will not stay that way.
  • Exchanges are hungry for new projects. That shifts when the market heats up.
  • Competitors are waiting. The ones moving now will own the narrative.

Step-Down Option: The Korea Market Audit

For leads who say 'we want to explore but $50K is too much,' offer the Korea Market Audit.

  • Price: $2-5K.
  • Timeline: 2 weeks.
  • Deliverable: A 20-page research report on their opportunity in Korea. Market size, competitor analysis, KOL landscape, exchange strategy, 90-day roadmap.

Why this works. They get clarity without full commitment. You get to show your work. Half the time, they upgrade from Audit to full engagement after seeing the report.

09. Objection Handling Scripts

Most objections can be prevented during discovery. If Jdot runs the CLOSER framework properly, 70% of objections never come up. When they do, the approach is: acknowledge, ask a deeper question, and let them talk themselves through it. Never fight an objection with facts.

We need to think about it

Jdot
Response:
Absolutely. It is a big decision. What specifically do you want to think through? I might be able to help clarify right now.

Move. If they cannot name what they want to think about, the blocker is emotional, not logical. Ask deeper: 'Is it timing? Is it the scope? Is it the investment?' Let them land on it.

Timing isn't right / Bear market

Jdot
Response:
I hear you. Most teams feel the same pressure. The ones who moved forward in this market actually have an advantage. Less noise, lower KOL costs, exchanges more open to partnerships. But I am not going to push you on timing. I will keep you posted on what is happening.

Note. Acknowledge. Reframe the bear market as an advantage. Then REMOVE the pressure. Move to nurture.

Too expensive

Jdot
Response:
What is the Korean market worth to your token over the next 12 months? If volume increased 30-40% from Korean exchanges alone, what does that look like in dollar terms? ... Our fee is a fraction of that upside. But if the full scope is too much to start, we have a Korea Market Audit that gives you a concrete picture before committing to the full thing.

Talking to other agencies

Jdot
Response:
Good. You should be. A few things to look for when comparing. ask them about direct relationships with Korean exchanges. Ask for specific results in your category. And ask if they operate in-country. There is a big difference between a remote team saying they 'do Korea' and a team that is actually in Seoul. Happy to answer those same questions right now.

We can do this in-house

Jdot
Response:
Some teams do. The challenge we have seen is that it takes 6-12 months to build the relationships and cultural knowledge you would need. That is with a full-time Korean-speaking hire. Most teams end up spending more on the hire plus the learning curve. But if you go that route, happy to share what to look for in a Korean market lead.

Burned by agencies before

Jdot
Response:
Yeah, crypto is full of agencies that overpromise. I won't pretend otherwise. The best I can do is show you results. [reference case study]. And if trust is the concern, the Korea Market Audit is designed exactly for that. You see our work quality before committing to anything bigger.

Korea isn't a priority

Jdot
Response:
Makes sense. I will keep you in the loop on what is happening in Korea for [their category]. If a competitor moves or the landscape shifts, you will want to know even if you are not acting on it.

NEW: I need to talk to my partner / cofounder / team

Jdot
Response:
Totally get it. Who else is involved in this decision? ... Happy to jump on a quick call with them to walk through it. Usually makes it easier than relaying everything. Can we set that up for later this week?

Move. This is where decision-maker mapping from discovery pays off. If you already know who the other stakeholders are, you can be specific. 'Want me to set up a 20-min call with [name] and walk them through it?' BAMFAM. Book the call right there.

NEW: The Negative Close (For On-the-Fence Prospects)

When someone is interested but cannot commit, sometimes the most powerful move is the takeaway.

Jdot
Script:
Look, I want to be upfront. This is not for every project. Korea expansion is a real commitment and it only works when the team is ready to move. If the timing does not feel right, that is completely fine. We would rather work with teams who are all-in than try to convince someone who is not there yet.

Why this works. Hormozi and Klaff both use this. When you take the option away, it forces a real decision. If they were on the fence, the fear of losing access pushes them to commit. If they truly are not ready, you have saved yourself weeks of chasing.

Using the NEPQ Framework for Objections

When an objection comes up, use NEPQ to dig deeper.

  • N. Name it. 'So timing is the concern?'
  • E. Explore it. 'What specifically about timing is holding you back?'
  • P. Probe deeper. 'If we could start in 60 days instead of 30, would that change things?'
  • Q. Quote them. 'So what I am hearing is you need 60 days before you can move. Is that right?'

The 7-Step Framework

Everything in this playbook compresses to seven steps.

  1. Phil sends DMs, gets responses, books discovery calls with Jdot.
  2. Jdot preps (10 min research). Sends agenda 24-48 hrs before.
  3. Discovery call. Diagnose. Share insights. Map decision-makers. Plant soundbites. End with BAMFAM.
  4. BAMFAM. Book the next meeting before hanging up.
  5. Step 5A (Bucket A). Proposal call already booked. Close in 1.2 weeks.
  6. Step 5B (Bucket B). Data walkthrough already booked. 2.3 value touches over 21 days. Proposal when warm.
  7. Step 5C (Bucket C). Monthly nurture. One value message per month. Watch for triggers. When a lead warms up, go to Step 5A or 5B.

Every proposal call ends with a booked next step. Contract review, team call, or decision date. BAMFAM.

Every month. Watch all Bucket B and C leads for trigger events. When something changes for them. When a competitor moves. When they fundraise. Jdot re-engages directly and starts the cycle again at Step 3.

This is the playbook. No guesswork. Every step has a script. Every call has a framework. Every message is written. Use it. Repeat it. Watch the deals flow.
